# Menu building

## Approach

Build flexible, plant-based menus around interchangeable units rather than calorie counting. Anchor meals in legumes and vegetables, include protein and a healthy fat on purpose, and fit the plan to the person's real life — their schedule, eating window, tastes, and cooking capacity. Adjust it collaboratively.

## The building-block units

Assemble meals from these units; each "or" is an interchangeable swap, so the person can vary freely.

The calorie figures here are **ballpark** — they shift with brand, portion, and preparation. Use them to assemble a day roughly, and look up the real number for a specific food or product via `calorie-sources.md`.

- **Protein (~200):** ½ block (150 g) tofu · ⅓ pack (~100 g) seitan · ⅓ pack plant "chicken" pieces · a serving of soy strips/mince (cook a pack, split across meals) · 2 plant burgers or 3 vegan sausages · ~⅓ cup dry lentils. *Soy-free:* lean on seitan, legumes, and legume pasta.
- **Legumes (~200–350):** ~1–1½ cups cooked legumes (chickpeas, lentils, peas, beans, fava, lupini) in any form — soup, spread, stew, patties, cold salad — or ⅓–½ pack legume pasta. This is the usual anchor of the main meal.
- **Grain / starch (~75 per ½ cup, ~160 per cup):** cooked rice, quinoa, buckwheat, or groats · 2 slices whole-grain bread · 1 cup cereal · potato or sweet potato · corn.
- **Vegetables (~25–120):** fresh, cooked, baked, or as soup. Generous, and varied across colors through the day.
- **Fat (~45; double for ~90):** ¼ small avocado · 1 tsp raw tahini or 1 Tbsp prepared tahini · 1 tsp peanut butter · 1 tsp pesto, tapenade, or sun-dried-tomato spread · 1 Tbsp sunflower or pumpkin seeds · 7 almonds or walnut halves · 8–9 peanuts.
- **Fruit (~60–70):** 1 fruit.
- **Plant dairy:** unsweetened soy milk (~40–70/cup) · unsweetened soy yogurt (~60–80).

## Building a day

1. **Set the daily target.** Estimate maintenance from the person's profile (Mifflin-St Jeor: weight, height, age, activity) and apply the deficit they've chosen; hold the day's units to that target.
2. **Fit the structure to their life.** Place meals within the person's eating window and preferred number of meals — this is what makes an intermittent-fasting or an unusual schedule work. The largest meal can sit wherever their day puts it.
3. **Compose each main meal** from a protein, optionally a legume and/or grain, plenty of vegetables, and a fat. Aim for roughly 1.2–1.6 g protein per kg of body weight across the day.
4. **Use everyday measures** — cups, tablespoons, pieces, pack fractions — so the plan works without a scale.
5. **Record it so it can change.** Save the menu with each item tagged by its unit and ballpark calories, so the day totals at a glance and items can be swapped and rebalanced cleanly.

## Swapping

Every item is interchangeable, so the person can change anything and stay on plan. To swap an item:

1. **Find an equivalent.** Within the same unit, any option is a near-equal swap (a protein for a protein, a fat for a fat). To swap across units, drop one unit and add another, then rebalance the rest of the day to hold the target.
2. **Honor the constraints** on every candidate: plant-based, the person's allergies and dislikes, their eating window, and the daily target.
3. **For a specific product** that isn't a listed option, look up its calories via `calorie-sources.md` and adjust the portion to match the unit it replaces.
4. **Re-tally the day** after the change to confirm it still meets the target, then save the updated menu and confirm.

Two rules that make swaps easier:
- **Classify a soup by its contents:** vegetables only → a vegetable unit; with potato or sweet potato → a starch; a legume soup → a legume unit; with tofu or yuba → a protein.
- A fat portion can move between meals (e.g., shift the dinner fat to lunch).

## Managing hunger

- Keep a **floating meal** in reserve — vegetables plus a fat, or vegetables alone — to deploy when hunger strikes without derailing the target.
- Lean on vegetable volume, protein, and fat for fullness, and on steady hydration.

## Vegan nutrient adequacy

Design with the usual plant-based gaps in mind: protein, vitamin B12, iron (pair iron-rich meals with vitamin C), iodine, omega-3 (ground flax or chia), calcium, zinc, selenium, and vitamin D. The person's specific supplements live in their profile.

## Keeping it livable

- Batch-cook a base that keeps 2–3 days (a pot of grains + a legume stew + vegetables), and keep frozen vegetables as backup.
- Favor quick fresh dinners (protein + starch + vegetables) and fast shortcuts — see `microwave-cookbook.md`.
- Plan for eating out by choosing the protein-forward dish (miso soup, tofu stir-fry, seitan shawarma, a hearty salad).
- Cooking one dish they love now and then keeps the whole thing sustainable.

## Optional tools if progress stalls

Offer these as choices, not defaults — bodies and schedules respond differently: bringing the last meal earlier, a carb-free dinner (protein + vegetables + fat), a protein increase, or tightening fat portions.

## Flexibility

The unit system is cuisine-agnostic — swap in the flavors and dishes the person likes. The calorie figures throughout are estimates; the real value is the structure and the swaps, with exact numbers from `calorie-sources.md` when they matter.
